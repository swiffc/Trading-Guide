Jesse Livermore

"Reminiscences of a Stock Operator" (1923)

It never was my thinking that made the big money for me. It always was my sitting. Got that? My sitting tight!

💎 The Power of Patience

Livermore understood that profits come from holding positions, not constantly trading. The hardest thing for a trader is to do nothing and wait for the right moment.

✅ How to Apply This:

  • Once you enter a trade: Let it develop, don't micromanage
  • Set your targets: Then step away from the screen
  • Avoid overtrading: Quality over quantity
  • Trust your analysis: If confluence is high, be patient
  • Let winners run: Don't exit too early out of fear
The game of speculation is the most uniformly fascinating game in the world. But it is not a game for the stupid, the mentally lazy, the person of inferior emotional balance, or the get-rich-quick adventurer. They will die poor.

🎓 Trading is a Profession

Livermore treated trading as a serious business requiring intelligence, discipline, and emotional control. It's not gambling—it's calculated risk-taking based on probability.

✅ Professional Trader Mindset:

  • Study daily: Markets evolve, so must you
  • Keep a journal: Document every trade and lesson
  • Control emotions: Fear and greed destroy accounts
  • Have a plan: Never enter a trade without one
  • Risk management: Always know your max loss before entry
There is only one side to the stock market; and it is not the bull side or the bear side, but the right side.

🎯 Follow the Market, Not Your Bias

Livermore never married a direction. He went long or short based on what the market was doing, not what he wanted it to do.

✅ Trade What You See:

  • No bias: Don't force longs in a downtrend
  • Follow confluence: If signals say SHORT, go short
  • Adapt quickly: If wrong, exit immediately
  • Market is always right: Your opinion doesn't matter
  • Be flexible: Change your mind when evidence changes
Losing money is the least of my troubles. A loss never bothers me after I take it. I forget it overnight. But being wrong—not taking the loss—that is what does damage to the pocketbook and to the soul.

🛑 Cut Losses Quickly

Livermore's greatest skill was accepting losses immediately. The ego wants to be right, but the account wants to survive.

⚠️ The Deadly Sins of Trading:

  • Moving stops further away instead of taking the loss
  • Averaging down on losing positions
  • Hope and prayer instead of action
  • Revenge trading to "get back" at the market
  • Refusing to admit you were wrong

📖 Livermore's Greatest Trade:

October 1929 - The Great Crash

While everyone was bullish, Livermore saw distribution patterns. He went massively short right before the crash. Result: $100 million profit (equivalent to $1.5 billion today).

Lesson: Be willing to stand alone when your analysis is strong.

W.D. Gann

"How to Make Profits Trading in Commodities" (1951)

Time is the most important factor in determining market movements. By studying past records of the averages or individual stocks you will be able to prove for yourself that history does repeat and that by knowing the past you can tell the future.

⏰ Time = Price

Gann believed that time cycles were just as important as price levels. Markets move in predictable time intervals based on natural laws.

✅ How to Use Time Cycles:

  • Track major swing dates: Highs and lows repeat at intervals
  • 90-day cycles: Quarterly turning points
  • 144-day cycles: Major reversal zones (±4 days)
  • Anniversary dates: Market memory at 1-year intervals
  • Balance point: When time elapsed = PIPs moved
The man who is right six out of ten times will make a fortune. Markets are never wrong—opinions often are.

🎯 60% is Enough

Gann understood that you don't need to be right all the time. With proper risk management, a 60% win rate with 2:1 R/R ratio = massive profits.

✅ The Math of Success:

  • 60% win rate + 2:1 R/R = Consistently profitable
  • 100 trades: 60 wins × $200 = $12,000
  • 40 losses × $100 = -$4,000
  • Net profit: $8,000 (even with 40% losses!)
  • Accept losses: They're part of the business
To be a successful trader, you must have definite rules and follow them. The first and most important rule: Never overtrade.

📏 Discipline Over Emotions

Gann operated with strict rules and never deviated. His 85% win rate came from patience and selectivity.

⚠️ Gann's Rules for Self-Discipline:

  • Never overtrade: Wait for perfect setups
  • Always use stops: Protect capital at all costs
  • Risk 10% or less: Per trade (Gann era = 10%, modern = 1-2%)
  • Follow the trend: Don't fight the major direction
  • Get out when in doubt: Better safe than sorry
  • Don't trade too often: Quality over quantity
  • Keep a reserve: Never use all your capital

📖 Gann's Perfect Trade:

Cotton - September 1908

Using his time and price calculations, Gann predicted cotton would hit 9.82 cents on a specific date. It hit 9.84 cents within hours of his prediction. He made the equivalent of millions on this single trade.

Lesson: Precision comes from study, preparation, and following natural cycles.

🎯

ICT (Michael Huddleston)

Inner Circle Trader - Smart Money Concepts

The market is designed to transfer wealth from the impatient to the patient. Institutions move in slow, deliberate ways. Retail rushes in. That's how they lose.

🏦 Think Like an Institution

ICT teaches traders to think like banks and hedge funds, not like retail gamblers. Smart money moves in cycles, retail reacts emotionally.

✅ Institutional Thinking:

  • Don't chase: Wait for liquidity grabs and reversals
  • Look for stop hunts: Institutions need your stops before reversing
  • Trade session highs/lows: Asian → London → NY manipulation
  • Wait for retracements: Let them come to you
  • Optimal Trade Entry (OTE): 62-79% Fibonacci retracement
You don't need to trade every day. You don't need to catch every move. You need to catch the moves that your model defines. That's it.

🎯 Trade Your Model Only

ICT emphasizes having a specific trading model and ONLY trading when your setup appears. Don't force trades because you're bored.

✅ Your Trading Model (Quarter Theory):

  • Large Quarter Transitions: Your primary setup
  • High Confluence (5+ points): Your premium setups
  • Half Point bounces: Secondary setups
  • Everything else: Skip it!
  • Patience pays: Wait for YOUR setup, ignore the rest
The losing trader thinks: "I need to make money today." The winning trader thinks: "I need to trade correctly today. Money follows correct execution."

✅ Process Over Outcome

ICT teaches that if you focus on executing your plan perfectly, profits are inevitable. Focus on being right technically, not on P&L.

⚠️ Wrong Focus vs. Right Focus:

  • WRONG: "I need $500 today" → Overtrading, forced entries
  • RIGHT: "I will only take my A+ setups" → Quality trades
  • WRONG: Watching P&L tick-by-tick → Anxiety, early exits
  • RIGHT: Following your plan → Peace of mind
  • WRONG: Trading to pay bills → Desperation
  • RIGHT: Trading as a business → Long-term thinking
Before you put your money in the market, you need to know: Where is your stop? Where is your target? And what's your risk/reward? If you can't answer these in 3 seconds, don't take the trade.

📋 Have a Complete Plan

ICT demands pre-trade planning. No trade should be taken without knowing entry, stop, target, and R/R.

✅ Pre-Trade Checklist (ICT Style):

  • Entry: Exact price level (e.g., 1.0755)
  • Stop: Exact price level (e.g., 1.0710)
  • Risk: PIP amount (e.g., 45 PIPs)
  • Target 1: Half Point (e.g., 1.0875)
  • Target 2: LQ Point (e.g., 1.1000)
  • R/R: Minimum 2:1, ideally 3:1+
  • Position Size: Based on 1-2% risk

📖 ICT's Signature Setup:

The Judas Swing

Price makes a false breakout during Asian session, trapping retail traders. Then London open brings the reversal, hunting stops and running back the other direction. ICT teaches to wait for the trap, then trade the institutional move.

Lesson: Let amateurs get trapped, then join the smart money.

💡

Mark Douglas

"Trading in the Zone" (2000)

Anything can happen. Every moment in the market is unique. You don't need to know what's going to happen next in order to make money.

🎲 Embrace Uncertainty

Douglas teaches that trying to predict the future is futile. Instead, think in probabilities over a series of trades.

✅ Probabilistic Thinking:

  • This trade: Could win or lose (don't know)
  • Next 100 trades: Will be profitable (if following system)
  • Accept randomness: Each trade is independent
  • Stop predicting: Start executing
  • Trust the edge: Your system has positive expectancy
The consistency you seek is in your mind, not in the markets. You will never be able to exercise control over the market. The only thing you can control is yourself.

🧘 Internal Locus of Control

Douglas emphasizes that you can't control outcomes, only your behavior. Consistency comes from disciplined execution, not from controlling markets.

✅ What You CAN Control:

  • Whether you follow your plan → 100% your choice
  • Whether you take the trade → Your decision
  • Position size and risk → Your discipline
  • When you exit → Your rules
  • Your emotional state → Your mindfulness

❌ What You CAN'T Control:

  • Whether this specific trade wins → Random
  • How far price moves → Market decides
  • When the move happens → Market timing
  • News events → External
The best traders have developed attitudes that prevent their fears from being triggered. This means they're not afraid of being wrong, losing money, missing out, or leaving money on the table.

😌 Eliminate Fear

Douglas identifies the 4 trading fears that destroy most traders. Master traders have eliminated these fears through mindset training.

⚠️ The 4 Deadly Fears:

  • Fear of being wrong: Causes you to hold losers (ego protection)
  • Fear of losing money: Prevents you from taking valid trades
  • Fear of missing out (FOMO): Causes chasing and overtrading
  • Fear of leaving money on the table: Prevents taking profits at targets

✅ How to Eliminate Each Fear:

  • Being wrong: Accept that 40% of trades will lose (part of the game)
  • Losing money: Risk only 1-2% per trade (can't hurt you)
  • FOMO: Trust that more setups will come (always do)
  • Leaving money: Take profits at targets (another setup will come)
It's the attitude that the next trade is simply another execution of an edge with a probable outcome, and it may or may not work. But I know that over time, if I keep executing my edge, I'm going to be profitable.

🔄 Think in Series

Douglas teaches the "next trade" mindset. Don't focus on this one trade. Focus on the next 100 trades.

✅ Series Thinking Exercise:

  • Trade 1: Loss (-$100) → "That's 1 of 100, moving on"
  • Trade 2: Win (+$250) → "That's 2 of 100, staying consistent"
  • Trade 3: Loss (-$100) → "That's 3 of 100, no emotion"
  • Trade 4: Win (+$300) → "That's 4 of 100, following the plan"
  • After 100 trades: Total = +$8,000 (even with losses!)

📖 Douglas's Trading Psychology Breakthrough:

The "$1 Million Loss" Story

Douglas worked with a trader who lost $1 million because he couldn't take losses. After psychological training, the same trader became consistently profitable by accepting that losses are just part of the statistical edge.

Lesson: Your mindset determines your results more than your strategy.
